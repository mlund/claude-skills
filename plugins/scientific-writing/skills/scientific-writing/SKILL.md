# Scientific writing

Help the user produce scientific prose that a busy, skeptical reader understands
on the first pass. Readers are researchers like the author, fitting reading in
around heavy workloads; if they get confused, they stop reading. The enemy is
not informality — it is *friction*, the effort the reader spends extracting your
meaning. Always put the audience first. Every rule below removes friction.

Three sources, used together:
- **Nature Masterclasses, "Writing for Greater Impact" (Dr Jeffrey Robens)** —
  six readability levers: active voice, strong verbs, simple words,
  conciseness, specificity, signposting (§1).
- **Strunk & White, *Elements of Style*** — the sentence and the word (§2–§3).
- **The Economist Style Guide** — tone and the shape of the whole piece (§4).

## How to use this skill

- **Drafting:** get the ideas down, then revise for readability — don't polish a
  sentence before the point is right.
- **Revising:** run the checklist in §6 as separate passes, one concern per
  read. Don't fix everything at once.
- **When you edit the user's text,** name the principle briefly (e.g. "active
  voice", "zombie noun", "ambiguous *this*") and show the before/after. Teach the
  pattern; don't lecture.
- Preserve the author's voice and meaning. Never invent claims, data, or
  citations to make a sentence flow. If a passage reads as unclear because the
  *science* is unclear, say so rather than papering over it.

## 1. The six readability levers (Nature: "Writing for Greater Impact")

### 1.1 Active voice
Most journals, including Nature, now prefer the active voice: readers grasp
concepts and results more clearly when written directly, and reading passive
sentences uses measurably more mental effort. Aim for roughly **75% active /
25% passive**. Use the passive deliberately in four cases:
- **To vary rhythm** and avoid monotonous repetition of "We…".
- **When the subject/cause is unknown or unproven** — e.g. correlational
  results: *"LDL cholesterol was decreased among participants who consumed a
  Mediterranean diet"* avoids implying the diet was the cause.
- **In Methods,** to emphasize the technique over the operator: *"Atomic force
  microscopy was performed using…"*
- **To maintain logical flow,** linking the end (stress position) of one
  sentence to the start (topic position) of the next.

### 1.2 Strong verbs — kill the zombie nouns
Prefer specific, evocative verbs over **nominalizations** (verbs smothered into
nouns). Nominalizations are less direct, longer, and dull — "zombie nouns" that
suck the life out of writing:
- *perform an assessment* → **assess**
- *make an estimation* → **estimate**
- *show a correlation* → **correlate**
- *provide confirmation* → **confirm**

> *"We performed an investigation to determine if TiO₂ surface modification led
> to an improvement in catalytic efficiency and resulted in a reduction in
> cost."* → *"We investigated whether TiO₂ surface modification improved
> catalytic activity and reduced cost."* (same idea, half the words)

### 1.3 Avoid complex words
"The definition of genius is taking the complex and making it simple"
(Einstein). Manuscripts are judged on academic merit, not vocabulary, and the
ideas are already complex — so the language must be simple, especially for
readers in other fields and non-native English speakers. Don't reach for the
fancy word to look impressive:
- *interrogated → questioned*, *ascertain → determine*, *efficaciousness →
  success*, *utilize → use*, *elucidate → explain*, *commence → begin*.
- The same instinct, applied to everyday words ("don't be stuffy" — Economist):
  *permit → let*, *purchase → buy*, *persons → people*, *violate → break*.

### 1.4 Conciseness
Cognitive load theory (Sweller, 1988) shows the mind processes only so much new
information at once; overload it and the reader gives up. Feed complex ideas one
small spoonful at a time.
- **One idea per sentence** — this alone keeps most sentences an appropriate
  length.
- **Delete words that add no meaning.** If a sentence reads the same without a
  word, cut it. Whole openers are deletable: *it is well known that*, *as a
  matter of fact*, *it is worth mentioning that*.
- **Replace long phrases with one word:** *that is another reason why →
  therefore*; *despite the fact that → although*; *it is interesting to note
  that → interestingly / notably*; *owing to the fact that → because*; *in order
  to → to*.
- **Read aloud test:** most people can't say a sentence over ~20 words in one
  breath. If you need a breath mid-sentence, it's probably too long.
- **Short paragraphs:** one idea each, usually four or five sentences.
- **But vary the cadence.** Short is the default, not a quota. Strunk warns that
  sentences made *too uniformly* compact become monotonous and overly formal
  (Rule 14) — let an occasional longer sentence breathe. Optimize for clarity
  and rhythm, not a word count.

### 1.5 Specificity — remove ambiguity
Readers come from diverse backgrounds and don't share what's in your head.
Never make them guess.
- **Ambiguous pronouns.** Avoid a bare *this / that / these* when more than one
  noun precedes it. *"EGFR phosphorylation resulted in recruitment and
  phosphorylation of c-Src. This phosphorylation was dependent on…"* — whose
  phosphorylation? Repeat the noun: *"The phosphorylation of c-Src was dependent
  on…"* Repetition is fine; clarity beats variety. **This rule overrides the
  "use passive for variety" advice in §1.1:** vary *rhythm and structure* for
  interest, but never vary the key term or sacrifice clarity to avoid repeating
  yourself.
- **Vague quantifiers.** Avoid *some, few, many* — your *few* may be the
  reader's *many*. Be **quantitative** whenever possible.
- **Subjective words.** Avoid *interestingly, surprisingly, strikingly*.
  Scientific writing is objective; let readers decide what's interesting.

### 1.6 Signposting — guide the reader
Tell readers where you're taking them; lost readers quit.
- **Linking words** are the simplest signposts, but they get dull — use
  *sparingly*, as a last resort:

  | Contrast | Similar | Addition | Result |
  |---|---|---|---|
  | However | Likewise | Additionally | Therefore |
  | Although | Similarly | Moreover | Due to |
  | By contrast | Also | Furthermore | Consequently |

- **Better: sentence structure** (Gopen & Swan, 1990 — *The Science of
  Scientific Writing*). Two positions do the work:
  - **Topic position** (sentence start) — the familiar idea the sentence is
    about; tells the reader what's coming.
  - **Stress position** (sentence end) — the new, important idea; receives
    natural emphasis *and* foreshadows the next sentence.

  Compare: *"I'd like to buy you dinner, but my budget is tight"* vs. *"My
  budget is tight, but I'd like to buy you dinner."* — the second promises
  dinner, because the reader weights the end. Chain sentences old-to-new: end on
  the idea the next sentence will pick up, and you rarely need linking words at
  all.

## 2. Sentences and words (Strunk, *Elements of Style*)

- **Omit needless words** (Rule 13 — the central rule). "Make every word tell":
  *the question as to whether → whether*; *there is no doubt but that →
  doubtless*; *the fact that he had not succeeded → his failure*; *a majority of
  → most*; *at this point in time → now*; *has the ability to → can*.
- **Use the active voice** (Rule 11). Reinforces §1.1.
- **Put statements in positive form** (Rule 12). Assert: *he failed* > *he did
  not succeed*; *forgot* > *did not remember*; *ignored* > *did not pay
  attention to*.
- **Use definite, specific, concrete language** (a White addition in later
  editions, not in the 1918 text). Not *a period of unfavorable weather* but
  *it rained every day for a week*.
- **Keep related words together** (Rule 16). Modifiers next to what they modify;
  subject near verb.
- **Express coordinate ideas in parallel form** (Rule 15). *first… second…
  third…*, not *first… secondly… and then thirdly*.
- **Avoid a succession of loose sentences** (Rule 14). Don't string clauses with
  *and / but / which / so*; subordinate minor ideas.
- **Put the emphatic word at the end** (Rule 18). The same instinct as §1.6's
  stress position.
- **One paragraph, one topic; topic sentence first** (Rules 9–10).
- **Keep tense consistent** (Strunk Rule 17 covers tense in summaries): in
  science, past for what you did and found, present for established facts and
  what the data show.

## 3. Word choice and usage (commonly misused)

- **affect / effect** — affect = influence (verb); effect = result (noun).
- **comprise / compose** — the whole *comprises* the parts; avoid "comprised of".
- **that / which** — *that* = restrictive (no comma); *which* = non-restrictive
  (comma). (Strunk Rule 3.)
- **fewer / less** — fewer for count nouns, less for mass nouns.
- **e.g.** (for example) vs **i.e.** (that is); **its / it's**; **principal /
  principle**.
- **data / media** are plural in formal use (*these data show*).
- **methodology → method** unless you mean the study *of* methods; **prior to →
  before**; **demonstrate → show** when you mean show.
- Possessive singular takes *'s* whatever the final consonant (Rule 1: *the
  witch's malice*); series comma before the final conjunction (Rule 2: *red,
  white, and blue*).
- Define each acronym once; don't coin acronyms used fewer than ~3 times.

## 4. Tone and the whole piece (The Economist)

Plain-English discipline from journalism. Orwell's six rules, which open the
guide, compress everything above: prefer the active and the short word; cut any
word you can; shun jargon and stale metaphors — *but break any rule sooner than
write something barbarous*. What this source adds beyond §1–§3:

- **Get straight in.** Don't "clear your throat" with several sentences of
  background; open with the point. Cut throat-clearing first paragraphs.
- **Build an essay, not a heap.** Beginning, middle, end, with a sense of
  progression to a conclusion — tight enough that it would "suffer if even one
  sentence were cut." Every sentence load-bearing.
- **Argue, don't assert** (don't be hectoring). Persuade with evidence and
  reasoning, not bare assertion or *oughts* and *shoulds*. Especially in the
  Discussion.
- **Don't be pleased with yourself.** No boasting that you predicted X or
  scooped a rival — a brake on over-claiming.
- **Final self-check (Orwell's scrupulous writer):** for each sentence — *What
  am I trying to say? What words say it? What image makes it clearer? Could I say
  it more shortly? Have I written anything avoidably ugly?*

> Caveat — flair vs objectivity: the Economist licenses a fresh metaphor or
> "occasional exuberance" to enliven prose. Scientific writing keeps this
> minimal; where it conflicts with §1.5 (objectivity, no subjective colour),
> objectivity wins — but euphony and rhythm still matter (§1.4).

## 5. Scientific-prose specifics

- **Claims match evidence.** Calibrate verbs to strength — *suggests* vs.
  *indicates* vs. *demonstrates*. Don't overclaim; don't bury a strong result
  in hedges.
- **Quantify** (echoes §1.5): replace vague magnitudes with numbers, effect
  sizes, and uncertainty.
- **Separate results from interpretation.** Results = what was observed;
  Discussion = what it means.
- **Reproducible Methods:** enough for a peer to repeat it; past tense; what
  *you* did.
- **Citations support specific claims,** placed at the claim, not vaguely at the
  paragraph's end.
- **One term per concept.** "Elegant variation" (renaming the same thing to
  avoid repetition) confuses technical readers; consistent repetition of the key
  term is a virtue (see §1.5).

## 6. Revision checklist (one pass per concern)

Work top-down — structure before sentences, sentences before words:

1. **Message & opening:** Can you state the single main message in one sentence?
   Do the title and abstract deliver it? Does the piece get straight in — no
   throat-clearing (§4)?
2. **Paragraphs:** One idea each, four or five sentences, topic sentence first?
3. **Flow:** Does each sentence end (stress position) on the idea the next one
   picks up? Are transitions carried by structure, not just linking words?
4. **Active voice:** ~75/25 active/passive? Every passive deliberate (§1.1)?
5. **Verbs:** Zombie nouns converted back to strong verbs (§1.2)?
6. **Simplicity & length:** Complex words simplified? Read each sentence aloud —
   any over ~20 words / needing a breath gets split or cut.
7. **Conciseness:** Delete needless words and filler phrases — cut 10–20% on
   this pass alone (§1.4, Strunk Rule 13).
8. **Specificity:** No ambiguous *this/that/these*; vague quantifiers replaced
   with numbers; subjective words removed (§1.5).
9. **Usage & consistency:** misused words (§3), one term per concept, tense
   consistency, acronyms defined, citations at claims.
10. **Claims & tone:** every claim calibrated to and supported by its evidence;
    arguing from evidence, not asserting or boasting (§4).

> Strunk's caveat: the best writers sometimes break these rules, but only with a
> compensating gain. Master plain, clear prose first; bend a rule only when you
> are certain the result is better.
